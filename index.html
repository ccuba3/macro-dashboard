Oil surges past $100 on failed Iran peace talks and Strait of Hormuz blockade threat, compounding an already strained consumer amid sticky inflation and geopolitical escalation.

The macro backdrop is deteriorating rapidly as WTI crude surges nearly 8% to $104, driven by the collapse of Iran peace negotiations and President Trump's threat to blockade the Strait of Hormuz. With CPI already elevated at 330.3 and inflation expectations at 2.36% on the 10Y breakeven, a sustained oil price above $100 threatens to reignite the inflation cycle that the Fed has been fighting. The 10Y yield rose to 4.32% on the session, reflecting the market's repricing of the stagflationary impulse from energy costs flowing through to core goods and services.

Consumer stress is becoming the dominant macro theme. Consumer sentiment sits at a deeply depressed 56.6, and news headlines confirm that record gasoline prices are squeezing household budgets while some companies are already passing through war-related cost increases. With the Fed Funds rate at 3.64% and unemployment at 4.3%, the Fed faces a classic policy dilemma: cutting further to support growth risks stoking inflation, while holding rates steady allows the energy shock to erode real incomes and potentially tip the economy into recession. Retail sales at $738B will need close monitoring for signs of consumer capitulation.

Markets are showing a tentative risk-off posture but not panic — the VIX at 19.23 is elevated but not extreme, suggesting investors are still in 'pricing in' mode rather than full liquidation. Technology outperformed with Nasdaq up 0.14% as a relative safe haven, while Financials and Healthcare led declines. Gold at $4,750 remains well bid as a geopolitical hedge, and the weak dollar index at 98.96 combined with EUR/USD at 1.17 suggests global capital is diversifying away from USD assets despite the flight-to-quality narrative. The Swiss National Bank's 50bp cut to 0.5% underscores that the global policy divergence is widening.

⚠ Biggest Risk Today
A prolonged Strait of Hormuz blockade pushing oil sustainably above $120 would trigger a full stagflationary shock, forcing the Fed to pause or reverse rate cuts while the economy simultaneously decelerates.
📍 Most Sensitive Sector
Energy — directly exposed to the Iran conflict escalation with crude above $100, and any resolution or further deterioration of Hormuz tensions will cause outsized moves in energy equities and downstream consumer-facing sectors.
