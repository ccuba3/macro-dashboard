Iran war-driven oil shock pushes CPI to 3.8% as tech sells off hard while the Fed faces a stagflationary bind with unemployment already at 4.3%.

Markets are repricing the stagflation risk in real time as April CPI accelerated to 3.8% annually, driven heavily by the Iran conflict's impact on energy prices with WTI crude at $101.56 and up another 3.6% today. The Nasdaq 100 led losses at -1.8% while the Russell 2000 was hammered -2.4%, reflecting the disproportionate pain small caps face from both rising input costs and tightening financial conditions. The 10-year yield surged to 4.45% while the 2-year held relatively steady at 3.61%, steepening the curve modestly but signaling the bond market sees persistent inflation ahead alongside a Fed that may be forced to hold rates higher for longer.

The sectoral divergence is telling: energy (+0.7%) and healthcare (+1.8%) outperformed as defensive and commodity-linked plays, while technology (-2.9%) bore the brunt of the selloff as rising real rates compress long-duration valuations. Financials eked out a small gain (+0.4%), likely benefiting from the steeper yield curve and expectations of higher net interest margins. Gold pulled back modestly despite the inflationary print, suggesting the dollar's 0.4% rally — boosted partly by the SNB's aggressive 50bp cut to 0.5% — is capping safe-haven metals in dollar terms, though gold at $4,678 remains at historically extraordinary levels.

The macro backdrop is increasingly uncomfortable: GDP at $31.9 trillion and nonfarm payrolls at 158.7 million suggest the economy hasn't cracked yet, but consumer sentiment at a dismal 53.3 and rising unemployment at 4.3% show cracks forming beneath the surface. The Fed is explicitly pivoting focus to inflation as the labor market stabilizes, which in the context of an active military conflict and tariff-related supply disruptions means policy easing is off the table. Wheat surging 6.7% alongside crude oil points to a broadening supply-side inflation problem that monetary policy is poorly equipped to address, raising the specter of a policy error regardless of which direction the Fed moves.

⚠ Biggest Risk Today
An escalation or prolongation of the Iran war that pushes crude oil sustainably above $120/bbl, embedding inflation expectations and forcing the Fed into rate hikes despite weakening growth — a classic stagflationary trap.
📍 Most Sensitive Sector
Technology, because its -2.9% drop reflects acute vulnerability to rising real yields, and any further hawkish repricing by the Fed in response to persistent inflation will continue compressing the sky-high multiples embedded in AI-driven names.
